  "content": "<h1>Unpacking Allegations of Caste Discrimination in UPSC Civil Services Interviews</h1>\n<p>The Union Public Service Commission (UPSC) is the apex agency responsible for recruiting candidates for the Indian Administrative Service (IAS), Indian Police Service (IPS), and other high-ranking civil services. For decades, the UPSC Civil Services Examination has been the dream of countless aspiring civil servants, particularly from middle and lower-income backgrounds, aspiring to reach prestigious administrative positions. However, with the release of recent UPSC results, a contentious issue has resurfaced on social media: allegations of caste-based discrimination within the UPSC interview process. This analysis delves into these claims, scrutinizing the evidence presented and the systemic factors that might contribute to such perceived biases.</p>\n\n<h2>The UPSC Examination Process: A Three-Tier System</h2>\n\n<h3>Prelims: The Objective Filter</h3>\n<p>The UPSC Civil Services Examination is a rigorous, multi-stage process designed to select the most suitable candidates for India's premier administrative services. The first stage is the Preliminary Examination, often referred to as 'Prelims.' This is a screening test comprising two objective-type papers. Candidates are presented with multiple-choice questions (MCQs), and they must select the correct option, typically by blackening a circle on an Optical Mark Recognition (OMR) sheet. Crucially, the OMR sheets are evaluated by <strong>computerized scanners</strong>, a process that is largely automated and objective. This ensures that the evaluation of the Prelims is not subject to human discretion or bias, as the scoring is based on a predetermined key and machine reading.</p>\n\n<h3>Mains: The Deep Dive into Knowledge</h3>\n<p>Candidates who clear the Prelims advance to the Mains Examination. This stage is a comprehensive written examination consisting of several descriptive-type papers. Unlike the Prelims, the Mains papers require candidates to write detailed answers, essays, and analyses. These papers are evaluated by <strong>human examiners</strong>, typically seasoned academics or retired civil servants. While the UPSC aims for objectivity in evaluation, the subjective nature of descriptive answers inherently introduces a degree of human judgment. Allegations of bias often surface at this stage, with claims that the examiner's background or biases might subtly influence the scoring of answers, particularly concerning candidates from marginalized communities.</p>\n\n<h3>Interview: The Personality Test</h3>\n<p>The final stage of the UPSC Civil Services Examination is the Interview, officially known as the Personality Test. This stage is designed to assess a candidate's suitability for public service beyond their academic knowledge. It involves a panel of experienced individuals who interact with the candidate, asking questions related to their background, opinions, and understanding of contemporary issues. The objective is to gauge their leadership potential, analytical skills, integrity, and overall demeanor. This is the stage where the most serious allegations of caste-based discrimination are leveled, with claims that interview panel members, often from dominant castes, may consciously or unconsciously award lower marks to candidates from Scheduled Castes (SC), Scheduled Tribes (ST), and Other Backward Classes (OBC) to favor candidates from upper-caste backgrounds.</p>\n\n<h2>Allegations of Caste Bias: The Core of the Controversy</h2>\n\n<h3>The Social Media Uproar</h3>\n<p>Following the release of recent UPSC results, social media platforms became abuzz with discussions and accusations of casteism within the examination system. Numerous messages were sent to content creators and channels urging them to address the issue. The central question being raised is whether the UPSC intentionally deprives SC, ST, and OBC candidates of marks to ensure that candidates from 'Savarna' (upper-caste) backgrounds secure higher positions in the civil services. These allegations are not new but have gained significant traction recently, fueled by anecdotal evidence and comparisons of marks between candidates.</p>\n\n<h3>The 'Savarna' vs. 'Marginalized' Narrative</h3>\n<p>The core of the allegation is that the interview panel, predominantly composed of individuals from upper-caste backgrounds, exhibits prejudice against SC, ST, and OBC candidates. It is argued that this prejudice leads to deliberately lower scores being awarded to these candidates in the interview stage. This, in turn, is believed to manipulate the final merit list, allowing 'Savarna' candidates to secure top positions, including coveted roles like IAS and IPS, thereby perpetuating existing social hierarchies and limiting opportunities for marginalized communities.</p>\n\n<h3>Memes and Discussions</h3>\n<p>The intensity of the discourse is evident from the proliferation of memes and discussions on various social media platforms, particularly those focused on Dalit and backward caste issues. These platforms are actively debating the perceived injustices within the UPSC system, citing specific examples to support their claims and creating a widespread narrative of systemic discrimination. This public discourse highlights a deep-seated concern among certain communities about the fairness and equity of the UPSC selection process.</p>\n\n<h2>Examining the Evidence: Case Studies and Data</h2>\n\n<h3>The Apala Mishra vs. Riya Dabi Comparison</h3>\n<p>One of the most widely cited examples illustrating the allegations involves a comparison between candidates Apala Mishra and Riya Dabi. Despite Riya Dabi scoring significantly higher in the written part, Apala Mishra received 215 marks in the interview, while Riya Dabi was awarded 162 marks. This disparity in interview scores, despite Riya Dabi's superior performance in the written exam, is presented as strong evidence of caste bias, leading to Apala Mishra's higher final rank.</p>\n\n<h3>The Mathematical Discrepancy</h3>\n<p>The marks awarded in the interview stage, particularly the specific scores like 215 and 162, raise questions about the scoring methodology. Critics argue that the absence of a clear, objective pattern in assigning marks—such as a fixed number of marks per correct answer—makes the process susceptible to subjective bias. The seemingly arbitrary nature of these scores, especially the precise number of marks, fuels suspicion about how they are determined and whether they reflect genuine assessment or predetermined biases. The fact that the total interview score is derived from the combined assessment of multiple panelists further complicates the transparency of the process.</p>\n\n<h3>The Interview as a 'Personality Test'</h3>\n<p>The UPSC interview is officially termed a 'Personality Test.' This designation itself is a point of contention. Critics argue that this broad definition allows for excessive subjectivity, where personal preferences and unconscious biases can play a significant role. Unlike a purely knowledge-based assessment, a personality test can be influenced by a panelist's perception of a candidate's demeanor, communication style, or even their perceived alignment with the panel's own worldview, potentially allowing caste prejudices to surface.</p>\n\n<h2>Parliamentary Inquiry and Government Response</h2>\n\n<h3>DMK MP's Questions in Parliament</h3>\n<p>The intensity of these allegations prompted a parliamentary inquiry. He stated that the UPSC had clarified that the allotment of candidates to interview boards is entirely <strong>random</strong>. The system, he asserted, is designed to prevent any form of bias or discrimination. He further claimed that the interview board members are unaware of a candidate's caste or category, and even their Mains marks are not disclosed to them. This randomization, according to the government, makes it impossible for bias to influence the process.</p>\n\n<h3>Critique of the Government's Response</h3>\n<p>However, this response has been met with skepticism and strong criticism. This form requires candidates to provide comprehensive personal, academic, and socio-economic details. It includes information about their family background, educational qualifications, optional subjects chosen, and often, details that can indicate their caste or community. The DAF is reviewed by the interview panel before they interact with the candidate. This means that the interviewers have access to a candidate's background information, potentially including details that could reveal their caste identity, contrary to the government's claims of complete anonymity.</p>\n\n<h3>The Interview Board Composition</h3>\n<p>A significant point of concern is the composition of the UPSC interview boards. While the UPSC states that board members are selected to ensure diverse expertise, critics point out that these boards are often predominantly composed of individuals from upper-caste backgrounds. The absence of adequate representation from SC, ST, and OBC communities on these boards is seen as a major flaw. When the majority of the panelists belong to dominant castes, their inherent social conditioning and potential unconscious biases could influence their perception and evaluation of candidates from marginalized communities. This lack of diversity can create an environment where ingrained prejudices are more likely to manifest.</p>\n\n<h3>The Subjectivity of 'Personality Assessment'</h3>\n<p>The very nature of a 'personality test' is inherently subjective. Unlike a written exam where answers can be objectively graded, assessing personality involves subjective interpretations. While the UPSC aims to assess qualities like leadership, analytical ability, and integrity, the criteria for these assessments can be vague and open to the interpretation of the panelists. In such a subjective environment, personal biases, including those related to caste, can subtly creep in. The absence of a rigid scoring rubric for personality traits allows for considerable discretion, which critics argue is exploited to disadvantage candidates from reserved categories.</p>\n\n<h3>Lack of a Defined Scoring Pattern</h3>\n<p>A significant critique revolves around the absence of a transparent and standardized scoring pattern for the interview.
